As someone with a math/cs background, I find cryptography fascinating since it's a real-world application of interesting math. Cryptographic protocols are used in blockchains, digital signatures, encryption of wireless networks, establishing secure connections, and lots of other things. If you're like me, concepts stick a lot easier when they're presented in math-speak. Diffie-Hellman key exchange is one cryptographic protocol that I think is fairly easy to comprehend if you understand the algebra behind it.

Diffie-Hellman is a cryptographic protocol used by two parties for key exchange. Except I think that "key exchange" is a misnomer, since there isn't actually a key that's being transmitted from one part to another. The two parties instead cooperatively generate a shared secret by exchanging certain information.

As is typical in cryptography, assume we have two parties Alice and Bob, and suppose they agree on a finite cyclic group $$\mathbb{Z}_n$$ along with a generator $$g \in \mathbb{Z}_n$$. Alice and Bob can communicate over an channel, and begin the protocol by choosing random integers $$0 <= a, b < n$$, respectively. Then they multiply $$g$$ by these random integers to get $$ag$$ and $$bg$$. They then send $$ag$$ and $$bg$$ to the other party over the shared channel. Alice receives $$bg$$ from Bob and computes $$a(bg)$$ as her secret key, while Bob receives $$ag$$ from Alice and computes $$b(ag)$$ as his secret key. And since $$a(bg) = b(ag) = abg$$, Alice and Bob actual generate the same key. This is the shared secret key generated by Diffie-Hellman.

That's all there is to it. The protocol assumes that an adversary knows $$g \in \mathbb{Z}_n$$, and the adversary can also learn $$g^a$$ and $$g^b$$ by eavesdropping on communication between Alice and Bob, but computing $$a$$ or $$b$$ is still hard for the adversary. This is because the adversary has to solve the [discrete log problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_logarithm#Cryptography). All the adversary sees are two random elements $$h, h' \in \mathbb{Z}_n$$, and knows $$g$$, so it must solve the equations $$h = xg$$ and $$h' = yg$$ to learn the generated secret. Cryptography works because of known hardness proofs, or strong evidence that certain problems are hard to solve, and the discrete log problem is *believed* to be a hard problem (though researchers don't seem to actually *know* much about [its hardness](https://cs.stackexchange.com/questions/113124/is-discrete-log-a-np-hard-problem){:target="_blank"}).
